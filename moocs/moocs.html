Thread Title: Did Plato Forget To Consider the Cave Citizens?
Just dabbling in the Allegory of the Cave, and it occurs to me that the fire and the 'movers & shakers' manning the artifacts aren't the only realities in the room, so to speak. What about the prisoners themselves? Plato says nothing about their being dumb. Being human, wouldn't they talk to each other? And wouldn't they gain real, true understanding of another human through that interaction (knowledge!). Could we assume that each individual contains within him or herself a 'reality' actually much deeper even than a statue or urn? Is that reality deeper, even, than sky or tree? Let's face it, even up here on the surface, vision is fallible. Even the puppeteers don't get the whole picture.

Hi Meg, I think this is a fascinating question. If you have two people whose whole lives have been based on a lie, would they still be able to have a normal relationship within the confines of that lie? I have to say, I'm really not sure. On the one hand, I think of feral children. If two children were brought up by wolves, they might have a relationship, but it's not really a human one. And in the cave, the shadows on the wall are not real objects, but the projections of them - so everything the prisoners discuss is through words for things that don't exist. On the other hand, if the cave society is close to our own (and Plato hints that it is, given that someone could escape and ultimately make sense of what they're seeing), then relationships inside the cave might be like relationships inside the Matrix. Neo has conversations and relationships with people inside the matrix even if, when he wakes up, he realises that all the cars, buildings, and even the weather never really existed.

Instructor's Post
In the original allegory of the cave, Socrates does talk about what the prisoners talk about: namely, they argue about the shadows and have contests to see who can best predict what shadow is coming next. That is, the idea is that there is conversation but, because of the superficial subject matter, it never gets anywhere. Everyone is just watching the shadow equivalent of cute kitten gifs, I guess. Can Haz Shadowz? The thing that is ultimately problematic, I'd say - the sense in which maybe Plato forgot the cave citizens? - is this: if we are born troglodytes (cave dwellers) maybe it's unnatural for us to try to climb to the light. That is, Plato wants everyone to start seeing and thinking and feeling in a way that, in fact, people don't. This can seem admirable - lofty, ambitious. Or it can seem kind of cold and inhuman. 'Human kind cannot bear too much reality.' That's T. S. Eliot, I think. How can we understand that so it doesn't just sound like cowardice? Socrates can seem like Neo, in the Matrix - leading everyone to freedom. But Plato sometimes strikes people as a bit more like Agent Smith. He wants to escape from the Matrix, too. But his motive is a bit different. A lot of different responses to Plato polarize along this divide: do you feel you are getting an emancipatory tale of uplift and freedom? Or do you have a nervous sense that there's something a bit too ... machine-like? (that's not quite it) ... about Plato's ultimate vision.
